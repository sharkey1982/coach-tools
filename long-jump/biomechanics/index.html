Long Jump / Biomechanics Explained

The four phases — approach, take-off, flight, landing — with a diagram for each. The bottom of the page maps the elite picture onto your TALL → PUSH → LAND theme so you can see what you're keeping and what you're (rightly) leaving for later.

1 Phase · Approach Run

The Approach 35–45 m · 16–22 strides

The whole approach is about arriving at the board at maximum controllable speed, in a position to take off well. Posture progressively rises, speed builds, and the last two strides do something special.

Figure 1 · Approach run — posture rises, speed builds, penultimate stride dips, final stride stamps
Drive Phase (first 4–6 strides)Forward body lean — like a 100m sprinter out of the blocks. Building speed from zero.
Upright Phase (middle strides)Posture progressively rises. By stride 8–10 they're tall and running fast — not flat-out, but controlled.
Penultimate stride (the "dip")Deliberately longer and lower. Drops the centre of mass so there's vertical room to rise into take-off.
Final stride (the "stamp")Shorter and quicker. Take-off foot plants slightly ahead of the body — body rotates up over it.

2 Phase · Take-Off

The Take-Off ~0.12 s of contact

Everything that decides the jump happens in roughly one-eighth of a second. Take-off angle, body position, knee drive and arm punch all combine to convert horizontal speed into a launch angle of about 20°.

Figure 2 · Take-off — body tall, knee high, arm punching up, foot stamping down. The horizontal vector is far bigger than the vertical — that's why the angle is 20°, not 45°.
✓ ELITE
✗ COMMON ERROR
Why 20° not 45°?Because horizontal speed is huge (~10 m/s) and vertical is small (~3.5 m/s). The launch angle is just whatever vector arises from those two — about 20°. Trying for 45° would mean braking heavily into the board, which loses far more than it gains.
Foot ahead of CoMThe take-off foot lands slightly in front of the body. Body rotates up and over the planted foot. This is what creates lift.
Tall trunk = essentialForward lean is the single biggest distance-killer. The trunk should be vertical or even leaning slightly back at take-off.
Knee drive + arm punchFree knee drives up to roughly horizontal; opposite arm punches skywards. Both add upward momentum without slowing horizontal speed.
3 Phase · Flight

Flight Techniques ~0.85–1.0 s in the air

Once airborne, the centre of mass follows a fixed parabola — physics decided it the moment the foot left the board. What flight technique can do is control rotation and set up a good landing. Three styles exist:

Figure 3 · Three flight techniques — same trajectory, different rotation control
Sail techniqueThe natural, untaught shape — both knees lift in front, body sails through. Fine for beginners; you'll see it at your session. Distance suffers because body wants to rotate forward and there's no counter.
Hang techniqueThe body lengthens fully — arms overhead, legs trailing. The "hang" position counter-rotates the forward spin from take-off. Common at club and school-county level.
Hitch-kickThe leg-cycling style of virtually every elite male jumper. Carl Lewis used 2.5 cycles. Hardest to learn but the most effective rotation control.
What flight CAN'T doChange the centre-of-mass arc. Once the foot leaves the board, gravity decides where the CoM lands. Flight technique only positions the body for a good landing.
4 Phase · Landing

The Landing where 30–50 cm gets won or lost

Two phases: heels first (out in front), then hips drive forward through the heels so the body folds and the bottom sweeps past where the feet first landed. Done well, the landing mark is where the heels touched. Done badly, the body falls back — and the official measurement is taken from that mark.

Figure 4 · Landing — heels touch ahead of the body, then hips drive forward so the trunk folds through the feet. The bottom should never touch behind the heels.
Heels first, far in frontLegs reach out so the heels make first contact well ahead of the body. This is what extends the jump distance — the body's CoM is still over the pit when the heels strike.
Knees slightly bentNever locked straight (injury risk and they can't absorb force). Bent enough to act as shock absorbers, straight enough to keep the legs reaching.
Hips drive throughThe decisive action. The bottom must sweep forward past where the heels landed, not collapse back behind them.
Arms swing backCounter-action. Throwing the arms back/down at landing helps pull the trunk forward over the feet.

How this maps onto TALL → PUSH → LAND

Your three teaching words are not a simplification — they're a selection. Each one maps directly to the most distance-determining principle of an elite phase, with the rest deliberately left for later.

Tall
Maps to · Approach + Take-off

The single biggest distance-killer at every level — beginner to elite — is forward lean. "Stay tall" fixes the most-common error before it can form. You're not dumbing this down — you're going straight to the highest-leverage cue.

Push
Maps to · Take-off contact

The 0.12 second ground contact at take-off. The tennis-ball-on-football demo is a perfect mental model — quick, firm push = high bounce. The cue trains tempo and intent, not biomechanics.

Land
Maps to · Landing

"Feet forward, hips through" — the elite landing principle, in two cues a 7-year-old can act on. Stops the sit-back collapse that loses 30–50cm at every level of the sport.

Deliberately not teaching at this age

Take-off angle (instinct handles it), penultimate-stride dip (too complex, easy to over-coach), flight technique (sail is fine until ~age 13), measured run-ups, and any biomechanical numbers. They'll get there. This session plants the foundation.
